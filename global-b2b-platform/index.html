Case Study · UX Research & Design Strategy

One Interface Was Supposed to Replace Four. I Tested Whether It Could.

Testing whether a global redesign could replace four independently built regional platforms, before the organization committed to it.

Scott Faranello · UX Researcher & Design Strategist

Overview

At a Glance

Role
UX Researcher & Design Strategist (Contract)
Context
A global B2B ordering and account-management platform for a multinational medical device and healthcare company (name available on request), used by sales reps, hospitals, ambulatory surgical centers, and distributors across North America, Europe, the Middle East, and Africa (EMEA), Asia Pacific (APAC), and Latin America (LATAM).

Global qualitative interviews, expert heuristic evaluation, thematic synthesis, persona definition, moderated usability testing of Figma prototypes, screen-by-screen design critique.

01 · The Situation

Four regional platforms already existed. The plan was to replace them with one, untested design.

A global B2B ordering and account-management platform was live across North America, EMEA, APAC, and LATAM, used by sales representatives, hospitals, ambulatory surgical centers (ASCs), distributors, and internal product specialists to place orders, track shipments, and manage accounts.

Each region had built and maintained its own version of the platform independently, and a new project was underway to replace all four with a single, unified global interface. The working assumption behind the project was that one design could scale more cleanly across every region and role, without regional customization. That assumption had never been tested against actual user behavior or need.

Stakeholder Challenges

  • Four independently built and maintained regional platforms, each reflecting years of region-specific use
  • A new platform design, already underway, built by the dev team on the assumption that one interface could replace all four
  • Diverse user roles (sales reps, hospital buyers, distributors, ASCs, product specialists) with assumed but unconfirmed needs

02 · The Main Problem

The project assumed one interface would fit everyone. No one had checked.

The working assumption behind the redesign was that the differences between how different regions conducted business were minor enough that a single solution would suffice and would be adopted. But there were unanswered questions, like:

  1. What pain points were users of existing platforms experiencing, and did the new "NextGen" design account for them?
  2. What will regional users need that the new design hasn't accounted for?
  3. How scalable is the design of the new platform?
  4. Will regions accept the change and willingly adopt the new platform?
  5. What findings would meaningfully prove that one platform could work for multiple regions?

These questions had not been sufficiently answered, and this was what I set out to answer. The unification project couldn't move forward responsibly until then.

Product Challenges

  • Unvalidated scalability: the redesign was premised on one interface working globally.
  • Structural integrity: multiple platforms already existed, and were built and maintained independently.
  • Trust in the platform itself: a select set of users was already using the new platform, but had not been interviewed about their experience.
  • Global consistency vs. local relevance: recommendations from my research had to hold up across five countries and at least five distinct user roles.

03 · My Process

Test the "one interface fits everyone" assumption before designing it

Before touching a single screen, the real question was whether four independently built regional platforms could responsibly be replaced by one design nobody had validated with the people who'd actually have to use it.

Research Activities

One-on-one interviews: conducted 27 interviews with platform users across 5 countries and 3 regions (North America and Canada, UK and Spain, and New Zealand), spanning 7 distinct roles including sales reps, hospital buyers, ASCs, distributors, and product specialists, some already using the "NextGen" platform, and some still using their region's existing platform.

Heuristic evaluation of the live platform: conducted a complete heuristic evaluation of each regional platform, as well as the new platform, covering login, home page, account switching, cart, checkout, and order history.

Synthesized findings into recurring themes: created an affinity map organizing findings into themes spanning information architecture, trust, digital fluency, mobile experience, and dispute resolution.

IT collaboration: presented findings to the development team to discuss pain points related to technical issues and determine feasibility prior to launching across regions.

Secondary research: benchmarked the new platform against B2C, consumer-grade e-commerce experiences to give the team concrete reference points for what "good" looked like outside their own industry.

04 · Discovery

Team assumptions were directionally correct, but unearned until research confirmed it.

The interviews told a consistent story across every region and role, whether someone was on an existing regional platform or already using "NextGen."

Sales reps in Spain and hospital buyers in New Zealand described the same pattern in their own words: an order goes in, and there's no reliable way to know if it actually went through. Tracking information that's supposed to update in real time is outdated or missing. Backorder status is unclear until it's too late to plan around it. So users called their rep, called support, or sent an email to get a straight answer.

"I know you can do it on here, and I know some reps do. But there's just been a couple of times I've gotten burned where it didn't go through, and I kind of feel like I'd rather just know it's done, and I can cross it off my list than do it here and still be questioning in my mind if it went through."
"I find myself using [an internal tool] far more often than the [platform], because I just know it's going to give me the information I need."

That second pattern that showed up independently across multiple regions was users circumventing the platform in favor of internal tools they trusted more. The heuristic evaluation of "NextGen" reinforced the same conclusion, but from a different angle. Pain points centered on lack of consistency, standards, flexibility, and efficiency of use (the two most frequently cited heuristic violations), followed closely by aesthetics and visual design, visibility of system status, and error prevention.

These weren't regional design issues. They were structural ones, consisting of inconsistencies and confusion between what a user clicks and what they land on, unclear system feedback after key actions, and information architecture that didn't match how users actually thought about their workflow.

05 · From Insight to Design

Validating "NextGen" against what research actually found

I used the interview and heuristic findings to define what the "NextGen" design needed to solve for users, then moderated usability sessions on the platform's existing "NextGen" prototype screens to test whether it did. Several elements of the new design were resonating directly with the pain points my research had found.

Testing also surfaced gaps that the design hadn't yet addressed, several of which mapped directly back to the trust problem discovery had identified. One comment in particular pointed to a pattern that showed up repeatedly in both the heuristic evaluation and the usability testing: the "NextGen" design carried forward inconsistent terminology between what users clicked and what they landed on. Fixing the visual design without fixing the underlying information architecture and taxonomy would have shipped a better-looking version of the same problems.

Deliverables Produced

  • Global UX research report: research spanning all regions, with evidence proving that a single interface would work, but also that design mattered.
  • Heuristic evaluation of the live platform: an independent, structured account of what was actually broken, separate from any single region's opinion of the platform.
  • Screen-by-screen usability findings on the "NextGen" design: validated which parts of the new design were working and flagged which pain points the design hadn't yet solved.
  • Prioritized recommendations: information architecture, trust and transparency, mobile usability, and communication.
"Order status is absolutely helpful. I can see the status of my orders."
"Honestly, I like just the cleanness of it, compared to the current version."
"We are not notified automatically. We would probably be notified by our local rep."
"If it's back ordered, what is the expected date?"
"What is bookings? Is that a new word? I don't think I've ever seen that on a menu."

06 · Delivery Decisions & Outcomes

Handing off findings and risk, not a redesign

The research and usability findings shifted how the team framed the design of "NextGen."

What had started as a visual update to a single interface, built on an unvalidated assumption, became a project grounded in evidence around what specifically needed to be fixed before the new design could responsibly replace four regional platforms.

I delivered my findings, heuristic evaluation, and usability testing results directly to the project team, including an explicit accounting of the risks: the terminology and information-architecture issues the redesign hadn't yet resolved, and the trust problems that a visual refresh alone wouldn't fix. Based on that work, the UX designer began revising the polished screens in Figma to address the issues the research had surfaced.

My engagement ended at that point. The project team needed to regroup and decide, based on the findings, how to move forward, and I was not part of the project after that decision point. I don't know what the team ultimately decided or what became of the unification project overall.

Outcomes

  • Research across 5 countries, 3 regions, and 7 user roles gave the project team, for the first time, real evidence on whether a single interface could serve every region, replacing an assumption the project had been built on with actual findings.
  • The heuristic evaluation gave the team an independent account of platform issues, separate from the team's assumptions about what to design.
  • Usability testing on the in-progress redesign identified specific terminology and information-architecture issues before they shipped, and the UX designer began revising screens in response to those findings.

07 · Reflection

Being right by assumption is not the same as having checked

Based on my research, I determined that designing a new interface was premature before the project team knew the actual pain points users were experiencing and had found common patterns to address those issues first.

And although the assumption that a single interface could serve every region was correct, the team acted on it before knowing whether the core problems were universal.

My research brought that to the surface and spared the team an enormous amount of rework, and likely a substantial increase in support costs, had they launched without it.
